Paying for biodiversity or familiarity? Investigating distance decay and relative preferences for fish conservation. (under review)
Easily recognizable species are often used as so-called ‘flagship’ species to raise awareness and funding for conservation action, but this practice has been criticized for neglecting low-profile species with important biodiversity status. One component of biodiversity is the geographic distribution of where species live, with species that live in only one habitat being endemic to that particular habitat. This study investigates how respondents to a discrete choice experiment ascribe value to the conservation of five different fish species with one species being non-endemic to the study area and familiar to most respondents while another, much lesser-known species, is endemic to the study area. Results show that a substantial fraction of respondents with high willingness to pay for conservation has a stronger relative preference for the non-familiar species endemic to the study area. While the relative appeal of an unfamiliar species with an important biodiversity characteristic is not limited by geography, individuals who live relatively close to unfamiliar biodiversity may be among those more likely to value such species higher relative to more familiar substitute species.

At the limit of volunteerism? Swedish family forest owners and policy reform
Sweden has instituted many environmental laws in recent years, but the regulations on what family forest owners can do with their forest properties has changed little in recent decades. This study examines how forest owner opinions of current forest policy reveal challenges and opportunities that will need to be addressed in efforts to negotiate and institute policy reforms that impact Swedish family forest owners.
